# Formalizer — Sources

Academic grounding for the tone taxonomy and the 1-5 register rubric. Loaded on demand only — not part of the always-resident skill.

## Register theory

**Joos, M. (1962).** *The Five Clocks: A Linguistic Excursion Into the Five Styles of English Usage.* Harcourt, Brace & World.
- Grounds the **register level (1-5)** rubric: intimate / casual / consultative / formal / frozen.

**Halliday, M. A. K., & Hasan, R. (1985).** *Language, Context, and Text: Aspects of Language in a Social-Semiotic Perspective.* Deakin University Press.
- Frames each tone as a configuration of **field** (subject), **tenor** (relationship), and **mode** (channel). Most tones in this skill vary tenor; `bullets` and `accessible` vary mode.

**Biber, D. (1988).** *Variation Across Speech and Writing.* Cambridge University Press.
- Empirical multi-dimensional model. Most relevant: the **involved ↔ informational** dimension underlying the practical formal/informal distinction.

## Plain language

**Plain Language Action and Information Network (2011).** *Federal Plain Language Guidelines.*
<https://www.plainlanguage.gov/guidelines/>
- Grounds the **accessible**, **readable**, and **concise** tones. Specific recommendations: 15-20 word sentence average, active voice, everyday vocabulary, one idea per paragraph.

## Domain-specific

**Hyland, K. (2005).** *Metadiscourse: Exploring Interaction in Writing.* Continuum.
- Grounds the **academic** tone — particularly hedging devices ("may", "appears to", "suggests"), boosters, and stance markers in academic prose.

**Wydick, R. (2005).** *Plain English for Lawyers* (5th ed.). Carolina Academic Press.
- Reference for the **legal** tone — when archaic constructions serve precision and when they do not. Note the tension: legal writing values both precision (archaic terms with settled meaning) and clarity (plain English movement).

## Politeness and pragmatics

**Brown, P., & Levinson, S. (1987).** *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage.* Cambridge University Press.
- Grounds the **polite**, **diplomatic**, and **empathetic** tones. Key concept: positive and negative face, and the strategies (off-record, indirect, conventionally indirect) used to mitigate face-threatening acts.

**Rosenberg, M. B. (2003).** *Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life.* PuddleDancer Press.
- Grounds the **empathetic** tone — observation / feeling / need / request structure. Useful for support replies and difficult-message rewrites.

## Style guides (consulted, not bundled)

- **APA Publication Manual (7th ed.)** — for `academic` tone, citation-aware phrasing, third-person convention.
- **Chicago Manual of Style (17th ed.)** — for `formal` and `academic` tones in humanities-leaning work.
- **New Hart's Rules** (Oxford) — for `irish-english` tone (British/Irish spelling and idiom).
